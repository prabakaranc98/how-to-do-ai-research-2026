# Research Sprint Review Checklist

**Purpose:** A reusable checklist for reviewing every research sprint.

Each sprint should be reviewed twice:

1. **Technical review:** Did the experiment actually test the claim?
2. **Research-craft review:** Did the sprint improve taste, rigor, judgment, and direction?

The goal is not performative productivity. The goal is better research, better learning, better artifacts, and better next questions.

## 1. Technical Review

### Question and Hypothesis

- Was the research question clear?
- Was the hypothesis explicit and falsifiable?
- Did the hypothesis come from intuition and reasoning, not random permutation?
- Was the scope narrow enough to test?
- Did the sprint avoid drifting into unrelated side quests?

### Experiment Design

- Did the experiment actually test the hypothesis?
- Were the baselines fair and meaningful?
- Were there enough ablations to understand what mattered?
- Were the metrics appropriate for the claim?
- Were cost, latency, data quality, or deployment constraints relevant and measured if needed?

### Evidence

- What did the evidence actually show?
- What did the evidence not show?
- Were negative results recorded?
- Were failure cases inspected directly?
- Were surprising results treated as signal rather than ignored?
- Was uncertainty, variance, or statistical validity considered?

### Claims

- Are the claims supported by the evidence?
- Are limitations stated clearly?
- Is there any overclaiming?
- Could an external reader reproduce or critique the result?
- What would change our mind?

## 2. Research-Craft Review

### Hamming: Important Problems

- Was the problem important?
- Was it attackable, not just grand?
- Did the sprint move us closer to a high-value research direction?
- Are we keeping a live list of important problems?

### Neel Nanda: Sprints and Feedback Loops

- Was the sprint short enough to create feedback?
- Did we learn by doing rather than over-preparing?
- Did we write a postmortem?
- Should we continue, pivot, or stop?
- Did we practice fast skills: coding, debugging, inspecting outputs, getting unstuck?

### Feynman: Scientific Integrity

- Did we report what could invalidate the result?
- Did we expose weaknesses and alternative explanations?
- Did we avoid cherry-picking?
- Did we avoid cargo-cult research: the appearance of rigor without real evidence?

### John Schulman: Taste and Continual Progress

- Did this project have enough headroom?
- Did we keep useful experiment notes?
- Did we follow through long enough before switching?
- Did the work contribute to long-term research taste?
- Did we balance current execution with broader self-development?

### Andrej Karpathy: Outer Loop

- Did the result unlock a better next question?
- Is this direction fertile enough for a lineage of experiments?
- Could this become a clear "person who did X" research identity?
- Are we solving something worth solving, not just solving what is easy?

### Jason Wei: AI Research Skills

- Did we practice idea selection?
- Did we design and execute experiments well?
- Did we write clearly?
- Did we create an artifact that could have impact?
- Did we think about how others could build on this?

### Michael Nielsen: Habits and Environment

- Did this sprint improve our research habits?
- Did we take responsibility for blockers?
- Did we improve the environment, tooling, notes, or workflow?
- Did this build a unique combination of abilities?

### Simon Peyton Jones: Communication

- Is there one clear idea?
- Is the story understandable?
- Are contributions stated explicitly?
- Would a reader know why the work matters?
- Did feedback reveal places where the writing or framing failed?

## 3. Sprint-End Decisions

At the end of the sprint, decide one:

- **Continue:** evidence is promising and the next experiment is clear.
- **Pivot:** the direction is interesting, but the current framing is weak.
- **Pause:** more intuition, reading, or tooling is needed before continuing.
- **Stop:** the hypothesis is not useful, the area is not important enough, or the attack is poor.

## 4. Final Sprint Questions

- What did we learn?
- What did we rule out?
- What surprised us?
- What was unnecessary?
- What should be simplified?
- What should become reusable infrastructure?
- What is the next smallest useful experiment?
- Did this sprint improve both the research result and the researcher?
